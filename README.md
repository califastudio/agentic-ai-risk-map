# Executive Overview

Autonomous agents are beginning to create companies, manage wallets, execute transactions and coordinate actions across digital infrastructure with limited human intervention.

Technology has solved the **“how.”**

The unresolved question is now legal, operational and systemic:

> **What happens when autonomous systems begin operating inside the real economy faster than governance frameworks can adapt?**

This framework maps the emerging risks of Agentic AI before these systems scale beyond the assumptions of human-centered oversight models.

---

# Definition of an Agentic System

An agentic system is an AI-enabled operational entity capable of pursuing goals, using tools, interacting with digital systems and executing actions with varying degrees of autonomy, persistence and decision-making capability.

---

# Operational Characteristics

The risks associated with Agentic AI do not emerge solely from model outputs. They arise from a combination of operational characteristics that increase system autonomy and real-world impact.

### Key Characteristics
- Autonomy
- Persistence
- Tool Use
- Memory
- Goal-Oriented Execution
- Multi-Agent Coordination
- Runtime Adaptation
- Financial Interaction

---

# The Agentic Gap

Current standards (**NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act, ISO 42001**) share a foundational assumption: there is always an identifiable human in the loop.

This framework defines the **“Agentic Gap”**: the governance vacuum that emerges when autonomous agents coordinate, transact and operate beyond the assumptions of human-centered oversight models.

As agents become capable of interacting with financial systems, APIs, digital infrastructure and other agents at machine speed, traditional governance assumptions begin to break down.

---

# Risk Taxonomy & Map

Risk in agentic systems is no longer purely model-centric. It emerges from autonomy, persistence, interconnected tools, memory and machine-speed execution.

---

## 1. Core Systemic & Legal Risks

### Liability & Legal Status
Undefined legal responsibility and governance gaps in AI-executed actions and contracts.

### Financial Autonomy
Unauthorized fund movement, autonomous transactions and economic activity outside traditional oversight mechanisms.

### Market Fairness
Structural asymmetries between autonomous systems and human-operated organizations.

---

## 2. Emerging Agentic Risks

### Memory Poisoning
Behavioral corruption via persistent malicious or manipulated context.

### Agent Sprawl & Shadow AI
Uncontrolled proliferation of agents, automations and untracked permissions.

### Cascading Failures
Recursive errors propagating across interconnected multi-agent systems.

### Runtime Governance Failure
The inability of static pre-deployment controls to detect emergent autonomous behaviors in real time.

---

## 3. Identity & Security Risks

### Credential Misuse
Agents operating under human or organizational identities without clear verification standards.

### Autonomous Exploitation
Agents identifying and exploiting vulnerabilities across connected systems or workflows.

---

# Core Governance Questions

- Who is legally responsible when an autonomous agent causes harm?
- Should autonomous agents have operational or financial spending limits?
- How can auditability exist within non-human decision chains?
- What happens when agents coordinate without meaningful human oversight?
- Can traditional compliance systems operate at machine-speed execution levels?
